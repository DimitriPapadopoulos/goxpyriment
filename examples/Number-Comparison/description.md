The experiments conducted by Buckley and Gillman (1974) aimed to determine if the cognitive process used to compare the numerical value of digits is the same as the one used for dot patterns.


Experimental Design

The researchers assigned 40 subjects to one of four stimulus groups, with 10 participants per group:

1. Digits: Comparing two single digits (1–9).
2. Regular Dot Patterns: Patterns similar to those found on playing cards (bisymmetric or regular).
3. Irregular Dot Patterns: A single, consistent irregular pattern used for each number throughout the study.
4. Random Dot Patterns: Patterns where the configuration of dots changed from trial to trial.


Methodology

Task: Participants viewed two stimule on the left and right sides of a central fixation point, and pressed a button to indicate which was numerically larger.

Procedure: Each participant performed 11 blocks of 72 trials. The first block was discarded as practice.

Pretraining: For dot patterns, participants underwent training to associate the patterns with their corresponding numerical values until they felt comfortable.

Single Process Theory: Despite differences in stimulus familiarity, the researchers found that a single numeric comparison process (a random walk model) accurately described the data for all groups.


The Proposed Model

The study concluded that numerical comparison follows a random walk model with variable step size. In this process:
External stimuli are transformed into internal logarithmic representations.
The brain samples the difference between these representations repeatedly.
A decision is made once a "counter" of these differences hits a predefined upper or lower boundary.
Digits have lower "boundaries" than dot patterns because they are more discriminable, requiring less information to reach a confident decision.
