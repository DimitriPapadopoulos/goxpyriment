To program a change blindness experiment, the stimuli you choose are just as important as the timing of the display. The most common method used in modern psychology is the Flicker Paradigm (popularized by Ronald Rensink).For your stimuli, I suggest focusing on these three categories depending on the difficulty level you want to achieve:1. Real-World Natural Scenes (Highest Ecological Validity)This is the "gold standard" used in classic studies. Use pairs of high-resolution photographs of everyday environments (e.g., a kitchen, a street, an office).Types of Changes:Add/Remove: An object is present in Image A but gone in Image B (e.g., a toaster on a counter).Color Change: An object changes to a different but plausible color (e.g., a blue car becomes red).Relocation: An object shifts slightly to the left or right.Pro-Tip: Focus on Marginal Interest (MI) vs. Central Interest (CI). Changes to central objects (the person in the middle) are detected much faster than marginal ones (a small plant in the background).2. Controlled Geometric Arrays (High Experimental Control)If you want to measure raw cognitive capacity without the "noise" of a real-world scene, use simple shapes.Stimuli: A $5 \times 5$ matrix of colored squares or "traffic signs."The Change: Randomly rotate one square by 90°, change its color, or swap its position with another.Advantage: This makes it easier for your AI agent to generate stimuli programmatically rather than requiring you to hunt for specific photo pairs.3. "Mudsplashes" (Distraction Stimuli)Instead of a blank screen between images, you can use "mudsplashes"—small, high-contrast gray blobs that briefly appear in random locations (but not over the actual change).Why use this? It tests if the brain’s "motion sensors" are being distracted by irrelevant visual transients, preventing you from noticing the actual change.Suggested Timing (The "Rensink" Formula)To make your code effective, use the following loop (often called the $A-blank-A'-blank$ sequence):PhaseDurationStimulusImage A240msOriginal ImageBlank/Mask80msSolid Gray ScreenImage A'240msModified ImageBlank/Mask80msSolid Gray ScreenImplementation Tips for Your AI AgentImage Alignment: Ensure Image A and A' are perfectly aligned to the pixel. Even a 1-pixel shift in the background will create a "jump" that breaks the effect.Pre-loading: Instruct the agent to "pre-load" all images into memory before the loop starts to avoid lag during the blank interval.Success Metric: The primary data point should be Reaction Time (RT)—the number of milliseconds from the start of the first flicker until the user presses a key.
